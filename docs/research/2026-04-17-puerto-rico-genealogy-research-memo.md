## 1. Historical Context: Yauco and Guánica, Puerto Rico (1800s–1950s)

### Founding History and Municipal Relationships

**Yauco** was founded by Fernando Pacheco on February 29, 1756 [CONFIRMED]. It served as the dominant municipality of southwestern Puerto Rico throughout the colonial and early American periods, with several barrios — including Guánica — falling within its administrative jurisdiction at various points.

**Guánica** has a layered founding history. Juan Ponce de León established an initial settlement at the bay in 1508, calling the location "Guaynia" after the Taíno word meaning "place with water" [CONFIRMED]. However, the town as a functioning municipality experienced several discontinuities. By the 1800s, residents of the Guánica barrio — then considered part of San Germán — petitioned to detach and join Yauco. That petition was granted in 1875 with support from the Arena brothers [CONFIRMED].

At the time of the 1898 Spanish-American War, Guánica was a small barrio within the municipality of Yauco, with approximately 60 houses, defended only by eleven members of the 4th Volante de Yauco militia [CONFIRMED]. This is the critical administrative context: records for Guánica families in the 1850–1913 period will almost certainly be filed under **Yauco** municipio, not Guánica. Guánica was not established as its own separate municipality until **March 13, 1914** [CONFIRMED].

**Most important finding for this project:** All pre-1914 civil records for Barrio Carenero residents will appear in Yauco's registry and Yauco's Catholic parish. This is the single most important administrative fact for research.

### Barrio Carenero: Geography and Significance

Barrio Carenero is one of the constituent barrios of Guánica municipality [CONFIRMED]. It is located in the southwestern coastal zone of the municipality, bordered by the Caribbean Sea and the Guánica State Forest (dry forest, now a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve). Key geographic features within Carenero include the Seboruco elevation. The population as of the 2010 census was approximately 2,670 [CONFIRMED].

The name "Carenero" derives from the Spanish maritime term for a careening yard — a place where ships were hauled ashore to clean and repair their hulls — strongly suggesting this barrio had historical significance as a coastal labor and maritime repair point during the colonial period [LIKELY].

For the Manning/Mestre family, Barrio Carenero represents the likely residential ground-truth for Silvia Belén and Pedro Mestre. Given that Guánica only became its own municipality in 1914, records for the Belén and Mestre families from before that date will be filed under:

- **Catholic Parish:** Parroquia Santísimo Rosario, Yauco
- **Civil Registry:** Yauco municipio civil records, beginning 1885
- **Census/Padrones:** Yauco census records

### Sugar Industry and Labor Context

The sugar industry defines the economic backdrop for this family's story. By the mid-19th century, Puerto Rico had approximately 789 sugar haciendas [CONFIRMED]. After 1898 and US annexation, the Central Guánica sugar mill — constructed beginning in 1901 by the South Puerto Rico Sugar Company of New Jersey — became the largest sugar mill in the Caribbean and, until World War I, one of the largest in the world [CONFIRMED]. It operated until 1982.

The Irizarry family is the **second most common surname in Puerto Rico** [CONFIRMED], with documented genealogical records linked to the Parroquia de Nuestra Señora del Rosario de Yauco dating to the late 1700s.

## 2. Puerto Rican Genealogy Research Strategy

### Civil Registry: Start Date and Coverage

Puerto Rico civil registration began in **1885** under Spanish rule [CONFIRMED]. The records cover births (nacimientos), marriages (matrimonios), and deaths (defunciones). The collection is on FamilySearch as "Puerto Rico, Civil Registration, 1805–2002" and on Ancestry.com as "Puerto Rico, Civil Registrations, 1885–2001."

### Records Before 1885: Church Records

The key parish for Yauco/Guánica lineage is:

**Parroquia Santísimo Rosario, Yauco** — Records available from **1751 to 1962** on FamilySearch [CONFIRMED]. Records are noted to be "very clear to read" compared to many other PR parishes.

**Neighboring parishes to check for overflow records:**
- Parroquia de Sabana Grande (north of Guánica)
- Parroquia San Germán (historically administered this region before Yauco's founding)

### Spanish Colonial Census Records (Padrones de Habitantes)

Available years held at the AGPR: **1860, 1871, 1875, 1880, 1882, 1888, and 1898** [CONFIRMED]

The 1875 and 1880 padrones are particularly valuable: these years bracket the transition of Guánica to Yauco jurisdiction and the years immediately after slavery ended, when formerly enslaved families like the Echevarría line would first appear as free persons of record.

## 3. Slavery and Emancipation Records

### Puerto Rico Abolition Framework

Slavery in Puerto Rico was abolished on **March 22, 1873** [CONFIRMED]. Approximately 29,000 enslaved people were freed, representing about 5% of Puerto Rico's total population [CONFIRMED].

The **Patronato system (1873–1876)** required formerly enslaved people to continue working for their former masters for up to three additional years. The Patronato formally ended in **1876**, two years ahead of the original schedule [CONFIRMED].

### The Registro de Esclavos 1872

The Spanish government ordered a comprehensive census of all enslaved people in Puerto Rico in 1872. Two related records are available:

1. **Registro Central de Esclavos, 1872** — FamilySearch Catalog ID: 177782; Ancestry Collection 2774 [CONFIRMED]
   - Content per entry: slave's name, country of origin, present residence, names of parents (if known), sex, marital status, trade/occupation, age, physical description, **master's name** [CONFIRMED]

2. **Slave Registers 1867–1876** — broader coverage, on FamilySearch

**Research Action for Don Juan Irizarry:** Search both collections filtered by municipality = Yauco, master's name = Irizarry. Any enslaved person listed under Don Juan Irizarry's household is a direct genealogical connection point to the Echevarría lineage.

### Naming Patterns After Emancipation

**Most important finding:** Many freed persons took the surname of their last master [CONFIRMED]. This is well-documented — surnames such as Franceschi, Oppenheimer, Arce, and Tricoche all trace to former slave owners.

**This is the most likely explanation for the Echevarría surname in this family.** If an Echevarría enslaver was the source of the surname, the original enslaved person adopted the master's surname, a common pattern.

The repetition of Echevarría across multiple family members (Benigna, Dolores, María Almodovar Echevarría) strongly suggests one progenitor adopted the name at abolition and passed it forward [LIKELY].

**Critical nuance:** A person who appears as "María" in the 1872 slave schedule may appear as "María Echevarría" in an 1876 church record. Do not expect exact name continuity across the 1873 abolition line.

### Tracing Don Juan Irizarry — Step-by-Step

1. Search the 1872 Registro de Esclavos on Ancestry (collection 2774) and FamilySearch (catalog 177782) for municipality = Yauco, master = Juan Irizarry
2. Note all enslaved persons listed under his name; record their ages, physical descriptions, and any listed relatives
3. Cross-reference with Yauco Catholic parish baptismal records (1751–1885) for any baptisms of enslaved children on the Irizarry hacienda
4. Search Yauco civil records post-1885 for the surname Echevarría to find the same individuals now appearing as free persons
5. Check AGPR notarial records for any land grants, wills, or property transfers bearing the name Juan Irizarry

## 4. Surname Origins and Regional Prevalence

### Echevarría [CONFIRMED — Basque origin]

- **Origin:** Basque Country. Derived from Basque *etxe* (house) + *barri* (new) = "new house." Found principally in Navarre and Vizcaya provinces [CONFIRMED]
- **Puerto Rico:** The surname is most numerous in Humacao (34%), Carolina (19%), and Naguabo (10%) in modern distributions — notably northeastern Puerto Rico, not southwestern [CONFIRMED]
- **Key inference:** This geographic distribution supports the hypothesis that the Echevarría name in this family was adopted from a Basque-origin slave owner in the southwest rather than representing direct Basque ancestry [LIKELY]
- **Puerto Rico colonial history:** Pedro de Echevarria y Veitia (1688–1763) served as governor of Puerto Rico [CONFIRMED]
- **Spelling variants:** Echeverría, Echebarría, Chavarría

### Irizarry [CONFIRMED — Basque origin, highly Puerto Rico-specific]

- **Origin:** Basque family name, an altered form of *Irisarri*, a habitational name from a town called Irisarri in Navarre [CONFIRMED]
- **Puerto Rico:** Irizarry is the **second most common surname in all of Puerto Rico** [CONFIRMED]. Documented genealogical records link Irizarry family members to the Parroquia de Nuestra Señora del Rosario de Yauco going back to the late 1700s [CONFIRMED]

### Mestre [CONFIRMED — Catalan origin]

- **Origin:** Catalan, derived from *mestre* meaning "master craftsman" or "teacher" [CONFIRMED via forebears.io]
- **Geographic concentration in Spain:** Catalonia (39%), Valencian Community (30%), Balearic Islands (16%) — definitively Mediterranean/Catalan, NOT Corsican
- **NOT Corsican:** The 403-family Corsican immigrant list for Yauco/Guánica does NOT include Mestre. The Mestre line is Catalan Spanish, likely arriving via the broader Catalan immigration wave to Puerto Rico and Cuba after Hispanization policies in Spain restricted Catalan language use in the late 1700s.
- **Puerto Rico:** 70 confirmed bearers (ranking 1,145th) — extremely rare, making the Mestre line highly tractable to trace. Any Mestre appearing in Yauco or Guánica records is almost certainly connected to this family.
- **Global:** ~36,079 bearers worldwide; 53% live in Europe. Also present in Angola and Cuba (Catalan immigration routes).
- **Research advantage:** Rarity means no name-collision problem. Find any Mestre in southwestern Puerto Rico 1850–1950 and they are almost certainly Keith's family.

### Belén [LIKELY — Religious/biblical origin]

- **Origin:** Derived from the biblical placename Bethlehem (Belén in Spanish) [CONFIRMED]
- **Critical research question:** For Silvia Belén, it is not entirely clear whether Belén is her maternal surname (segundo apellido) or a given middle name used as an identifier. This distinction has significant genealogical implications and should be **clarified first** in any record search
- **Research action:** Search civil records for "Silvia Belén" and variants to determine which position it occupies in the double-surname convention

### López [CONFIRMED — extremely common patronymic]

- **Origin:** Spanish patronymic derived from the given name Lope (Latin *Lupus*, wolf). One of the most common surnames in the Hispanic world [CONFIRMED]
- **Research implication:** The double surname "López Echevarría" means her father's surname was López and her mother's surname was Echevarría — her mother was in the Echevarría line

### Almodovar [CONFIRMED — Arabic/Moorish origin]

- **Origin:** Derived from Arabic *al-mudawwar* (the round one), a geographic/habitational name [CONFIRMED]
- **Family appearance:** María Almodovar Echevarría — father's surname = Almodovar, mother's surname = Echevarría, placing her in the same maternal Echevarría line as María Inés López Echevarría

### Spanish Double Surname Convention — Critical Research Tool

Standard format: **[Given Name] [Paternal Surname] [Maternal Surname]**

Applied to the known family:
- **María Inés López Echevarría** → father's surname = López; mother's surname = Echevarría
- **María Almodovar Echevarría** → father's surname = Almodovar; mother's surname = Echevarría
- **Both women share the same maternal Echevarría line** — confirming a single Echevarría progenitor in the generation above them [CONFIRMED by naming logic]
- **Benigna Echevarría and Dolores Echevarría** would then represent either the progenitor generation or sisters of that generation

In each generation, the maternal surname drops out. To trace matrilineal descent, you must find the marriage record of each generation, which lists both spouses' full double surnames.

### Interpreting Godparent Information

Godparent relationships (padrino/madrina) in Puerto Rican Catholic baptismal records are genealogically significant:

- Godparents were almost always close relatives — frequently the child's uncle or the mother's brother [CONFIRMED as genealogical practice]
- The godparent system created a formal "compadrazgo" (co-parenthood) bond — tracking these across multiple baptismal entries can reveal sibling clusters and extended family networks
- For enslaved families: the godparent listed was sometimes a free person of color, occasionally the enslaver, or another enslaved person from the same hacienda

**Action:** When you find a baptismal record for Benigna Echevarría, Dolores Echevarría, or María Inés López Echevarría, note every name in the entry — witnesses, godparents, officiating priest — and search each as new leads.

### Reading 19th-Century Puerto Rican Handwritten Records

Key challenges:
1. Letters "r" and "n" were often written nearly identically; "u" and "n" blur
2. Words frequently run together without spaces ("denombre" = "de nombre")
3. Common abbreviations: "Dña." (Doña), "Dn." (Don), "Leg." (legítimo/a), "Nat." (natural = illegitimate), "bca." (blanca), "pda." (parda), "neg." (negra)
4. Race classifications in 19th-century records: blanco, pardo/parda (free mixed-race), negro/negra, moreno/morena (free Black). These labels provide direct evidence of ancestry
5. "Hijo/a natural" (natural child) = born outside marriage. Expected for this lineage given slavery — do not interpret as unusual
